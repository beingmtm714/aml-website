The argument

This is not optimism. It's a structural shift — and it's happening now.

AI doesn't threaten artists.
Ignorance of it does.

As government arts funding is decimated and AI reshapes every creative industry, artists face a binary choice: become dependent on platforms and technologists who extract value from their work, or claim ownership of the tools themselves.

For decades, artists have relied on coders, labels, and investors to build the platforms their careers run on — trading equity for execution. That dynamic enriched technologists while disempowering creators. AI-assisted coding changes the equation entirely.

With the right training, an artist can now go from concept to deployed application — without a technical co-founder, without a venture round, without surrendering control. The psychological impact alone is monumental: the shift from aspirant to participant, from observer to builder.

We have witnessed this first-hand at Berklee. We are now bringing it to cities.

We are at the eve of a paradigm shift. With the right business training and AI-assisted tools, artists can now build, own, and deploy their own platforms — without a label, a coder, or an investor taking a cut. This is why the Lab exists, and why now is the only moment that matters.

A manifesto on creative agency, economic empowerment, and the end of the permission economy

01
The crisis
Decimation & disempowerment

Cities are losing
the one thing that
makes them cities.

Now more than ever it is incumbent — both morally and economically — for cities to foster a culture of empowerment and innovation. The argument isn't abstract. As governmental funding for arts is decimated across the country, cities that fail to find new mechanisms for sustaining their creative communities will lose them. And with them, they lose the vibrancy, the civic identity, and the economic engine that arts communities generate.

Arts are not amenities. They are infrastructure. And right now, that infrastructure is being defunded without a serious replacement strategy in sight.

Empowering artists and incipient entrepreneurs through focused, results-driven educational training is not just the best path forward — it is the only one. The alternative is to watch cities hollow out culturally while claiming to support their creative economies.

The stakes

When governmental arts funding collapses, the artists who leave are not the ones with industry connections and alternative income streams. They are the emerging voices — the producers, the songwriters, the independent label founders — the people who would have defined the next decade of a city's cultural identity — and contributed to the creative tax base that cities increasingly depend on for economic differentiation.

02
The threat & the opportunity
AI as both predator and tool

The same technology
that threatens artists
can liberate them.

The rapid advances of AI pose serious threats to artists' sustainability. This is not a hypothetical. Musicians, designers, and visual artists are watching their work used to train models without consent or compensation. Producers are competing against generative systems trained on their own catalogs. The asymmetry is stark and the outcome, if left unaddressed, is the systematic extraction of creative value from the people who generated it.

But the same tools can be turned in a different direction entirely.

Meanwhile, the old debate about whether commercial engagement constitutes "selling out" has been quietly settled by economic reality. Music licensing, brand partnerships, and direct-to-fan commerce are no longer optional revenue streams — they are the infrastructure of a sustainable creative career. The question is no longer whether to engage commercially. It is whether you do so on your terms or someone else's.

Artists have historically been reliant on others — coders, entrepreneurs, labels, investors — to develop tools and opportunities on their behalf. This dynamic has enriched technologists and disempowered creators at every stage of the music industry's evolution, from the era of vinyl distribution to streaming to the current platform economy. The story has always been the same: artists create the value; someone else captures it.

AI-assisted coding — what practitioners now call vibe coding — changes the equation. For the first time in the history of the music business, an artist with a clear concept, a defined audience, and a viable business model can build and deploy their own platform without a technical partner, without a venture round, and without surrendering equity or creative control to get there.

03
The proof
What we've seen at Berklee

From aspirant
to participant.
We've watched it happen.

The program director has taught artists business and technical skills for over 20 years as a Distinguished Professor of Music Business and Management at Berklee College of Music. In two decades of instruction, nothing has produced outcomes comparable to what has emerged in the past year, when AI-assisted coding tools were integrated directly into the curriculum.

The change is structural, not incremental. Previously, students would vision out their entrepreneurial and creative ideas — strong concepts, genuine insight into their markets, real understanding of the problems they wanted to solve — but because of prior technological limitations, those visions remained aspirational. They stayed in notebooks and slide decks. They required a technical co-founder, or a developer willing to take a revenue share, or capital to hire someone. Most ideas never became products.

Now, rather than visioning out their ideas and watching them remain aspirational, students are building and deploying. The shift in mindset and confidence that accompanies this is nothing short of remarkable. People who have long felt disempowered due to lack of resources or technical access are claiming agency — shifting from observer and aspirant to participant and builder.

The psychological impact alone is monumental. And the economic impact follows directly from it: artists who ship products have leverage that artists who don't, don't.

04
The structure
What we're bringing to cities

Concept to deployed.
In weeks, not years.

The Lab brings the Berklee model out of the classroom and into cities that need it most — tier-2 and tier-3 cities with strong arts heritage — music scenes, design communities, gallery cultures — and emerging creative economies that are currently underserved by the training infrastructure available only in major coastal markets.

Via the program, participants receive both the necessary startup and entrepreneurial skills and the AI-assisted coding tools to go from idea to deployment. Not idea to whitepaper. Not idea to pitch deck. Idea to deployed.

The program spans four phases: a pre-work period in which we partner with city officials to build a bespoke marketing presence and recruit the cohort; a boots-on-ground intensive in the city; an asynchronous development period of self-directed curriculum that leads to participants' MVPs; and a refinement and ship phase, culminating in an invitation reception for city stakeholders, community members, and sponsors at which participants present their completed projects.

The certificate issued at the end is endorsed by the program team, an affiliated university partner, and the city's cultural office. It represents a credential with real-world utility — for grant applications, employment, and the emerging economy of creative entrepreneurship.

The applications we build

All applications developed in the program must serve artists — helping them with career goals, business operations, audience building, or distribution. Some will succeed beyond participants' expectations and become ongoing businesses. Others will fail to work or resonate with customers. Both outcomes produce the innovator's mindset, the demonstrated capability, and the confidence that changes what an artist believes is possible for them.

Featured cohort — Mexico City, 2024
"For the first time, I understood my music as a business — not just a passion I was struggling to justify."

The Mexico City workshop was the Lab's proof-of-concept — the first full implementation of the program outside a formal academic setting. Eighteen emerging musicians, producers, and creative entrepreneurs came in with strong creative practices and limited business infrastructure. They left with functioning business models, revenue stream maps, and in several cases, the first deployed digital tools they had ever built.

The program ran over three intensive days, followed by async development support. The cohort included participants at varying stages of their careers — from recent music school graduates to working producers with established catalogs but no systematic approach to monetization. All 18 participants completed the program and received certificates.

The most significant finding was not revenue or product metrics — it was the psychological shift. Participants who entered describing themselves as artists began describing themselves as founders by Day 3. The vocabulary changed. The posture changed. The questions asked during 1:1 coaching sessions shifted from "how do I get discovered?" to "what's my go-to-market strategy?"

That shift — from observer to participant, from aspirant to builder — is what the Lab is designed to produce. Mexico City proved it can be done in three days.

Michael Martin contributes to the Lab's program architecture, strategic positioning, and the network of university and institutional relationships that give the program its credibility infrastructure. His connection to Brown University anchors the Providence relationship and opens pathways to academic co-presentation that strengthen the certificate's standing.

Michael's role is in the design layer of the Lab — the structure that allows the curriculum to travel cleanly from city to city without losing specificity or quality. A program that works in Mexico City must work in Pittsburgh or New Orleans with the same outcomes — and the systems he has helped build are what make that portability possible.

He also leads the Lab's broader advisory relationships, connecting the program to the creative placemaking ecosystem, philanthropic networks, and the policy conversations that shape how cities invest in their creative economies.

What we
actually
believe.

The Lab is built on a set of convictions about the music industry, creative economies, and what artists actually need. These are not marketing positions — they are the premises from which the entire program is designed.

They also define what we will and won't do as we build the program at scale. We are explicit about this because partners — sponsors, cities, universities — deserve to know what they're backing.

Principle 01

Artists are not assets to be managed. They are entrepreneurs, founders, and builders. The Lab treats them accordingly — with real curriculum, real accountability, and real outcomes.

Principle 02

The tools exist. The training hasn't caught up. Vibe coding and AI-assisted development have removed the technical barrier to building. The remaining barrier is knowledge — which is fixable in three days.

Principle 03

Tier-2 and tier-3 cities are not secondary markets. They are where the majority of American artists live and work, and where the gap between creative potential and business infrastructure is widest.

Principle 04

We will not partner with extractive AI companies. The Lab explicitly excludes music AI platforms whose business model depends on artist labor without fair compensation. This is a line we hold regardless of the sponsorship dollars involved.

Principle 05

The program succeeds when participants ship. Not when they attend. Not when they fill out surveys. When they build something real and send it into the world. That is the measure.
